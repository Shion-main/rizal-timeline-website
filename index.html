SS038 · The Life and Works of José Rizal

The Shaping
of a Hero

A documented study of the factors shaping his character, 1861–1896.

18611896

Thesis. This study argues that Rizal’s character is most defensibly explained by environmental and educational factors under colonial conditions, and that his status as national hero is itself a constructed, historically contested phenomenon meriting critical examination.

Dr. José Protasio Rizal Mercado y Alonso Realonda
I

Introduction

José Protasio Rizal Mercado y Alonso Realonda (1861–1896) was a Filipino physician, novelist, and reformist of the Spanish colonial period whose writings contributed to the emergence of Filipino nationalist consciousness.[1] He was born on 19 June 1861 in Calamba, Laguna, the seventh of eleven children of Francisco Mercado and Teodora Alonso.[2] He is conventionally designated the national hero of the Philippines; however, scholars such as Renato Constantino have noted that no statute formally confers this title, and that Rizal’s elevation was shaped in part by the American colonial administration after 1901.[3]

This timeline examines the factors that historians and biographers have identified as formative in Rizal’s intellectual and moral development. Following the analytical distinction set out in the assessment, it considers two categories of influence — biological inheritance and environmental conditions — before turning to the principal episodes of his life and to an assessment of his character. The discussion draws on standard biographies, notably those of Guerrero and Zaide, alongside Rizal’s own writings and the revisionist scholarship of Constantino and Ocampo, in order to present a documented rather than a celebratory account.[4][5]

Rizal’s character is best understood not as innate genius but as the product of inheritance, formation, and deliberate choice under colonial conditions.

II

Biological Factors

Inherited Traits

Rizal descended from a family of mixed Malay, Chinese, and Spanish ancestry, a heritage typical of the Philippine principalía of the period.[6] His paternal line traced to Domingo Lam-co, a Hokkien Chinese migrant from the Fujian region who was baptised in Binondo in 1697; the surname “Mercado” was subsequently adopted by his descendants.[7] It should be noted that the attribution of specific temperamental or intellectual traits to biological inheritance is interpretive rather than empirical: biographers such as Zaide describe Rizal’s father, Francisco Mercado, as industrious and his mother as cultured, but the causal link between ancestry and character is a narrative convention rather than a demonstrable fact.[4]

Rizal’s mother, Teodora Alonso Realonda, was unusually well educated for a woman of her time and is documented as his first teacher, instructing him before his formal schooling.[4] Contemporary scholarship, particularly Ocampo’s, cautions against the hagiographic tendency to read Rizal’s later accomplishments back into his childhood, and instead situates his early aptitude within the educational advantages his family’s status afforded.[5]

Physical Characteristics

Biographical sources record that Rizal was of slight build yet physically active, and that he displayed early facility in reading, drawing, and modelling in clay.[2][4] These capacities later informed his work in medicine, sculpture, and linguistics. As with claims about inherited disposition, however, such physical and precocious traits are best treated as documented observations rather than as explanations of his subsequent significance.

III

Environmental Factors

Most historians locate the decisive influences on Rizal’s development not in inheritance but in his environment: his family and its social standing, his formal education, and the structural injustices of the late Spanish colonial order. These factors are more readily documented and are emphasised in the major biographies.[4][8]

A. Family Background & Upbringing

The Rizal family belonged to the principalía, the local landed elite, and cultivated land leased from the Dominican order.[2] His elder brother Paciano, his senior by roughly a decade, is generally regarded by biographers as a significant intellectual influence and a conduit for reformist ideas.[4] Around 1872 his mother, Teodora Alonso, was imprisoned for approximately two and a half years on a charge that was later dismissed; biographers commonly cite this episode as an early instance through which Rizal encountered the arbitrariness of colonial authority, though the precise psychological effect attributed to it is an interpretive inference.[4][9]

B. Educational Experiences

Rizal obtained a Bachelor of Arts from the Ateneo Municipal de Manila, where he was rated sobresaliente, and subsequently enrolled at the University of Santo Tomas.[1][4] Biographical accounts hold that he pursued medicine, and later ophthalmology, in connection with his mother’s deteriorating eyesight.[6] In 1882 he departed for Spain, where he completed the degree of Licentiate in Medicine at the Universidad Central de Madrid.[1]

C. Social & Political Context

Rizal’s formation coincided with a period in which Spanish colonial governance operated substantially through the religious orders and in which Filipinos were subject to legal and social inequality. On 17 February 1872 the secular priests Mariano Gómez, José Burgos, and Jacinto Zamora (collectively GOMBURZA) were executed by garrote following the Cavite Mutiny.[8] Rizal dedicated El Filibusterismo to their memory, and historians widely treat the execution as a formative event for his generation, while noting that Rizal himself was eleven at the time and that his response is reconstructed largely from his later writings.[10]

There are no tyrants where there are no slaves.

— Attributed to José Rizal, El Filibusterismo (1891)[11]

IV

Life Experiences

A. Experiences Abroad

During his years in Europe, Rizal became associated with the Propaganda Movement and contributed to the reformist periodical La Solidaridad, alongside Graciano López Jaena and Marcelo del Pilar.[8] In this period he wrote his two novels, Noli Me Tángere (Berlin, 1887) and its sequel El Filibusterismo (Ghent, 1891), both composed in Spanish and now regarded as foundational texts of Philippine literature.[12][13] Scholars have observed that Rizal’s nationalism was articulated more fully during his residence abroad, where exposure to European liberal thought informed his critique of colonial rule.[5]

B. Life in Dapitan

Following his return to Manila and the founding of the reformist association La Liga Filipina on 3 July 1892, Rizal was arrested and deported to Dapitan, in present-day Zamboanga del Norte, where he remained in exile from 1892 to 1896.[10] Documented records indicate that during this period he operated a school, undertook a community water-supply project, practised medicine, and conducted scientific observation.[14] It was in Dapitan that he formed a relationship with Josephine Bracken.[1]

C. Arrest, Trial & Execution

In 1896 Rizal volunteered for medical service in Cuba; while travelling, and as the Katipunan uprising began, he was arrested. The standard accounts emphasise that he had no organisational role in the revolt and had expressed reservations about an armed uprising he judged premature.[1] He was returned to Manila and tried by a military court on charges of rebellion, sedition, and illicit association; historians generally characterise the proceedings as lacking the safeguards of an impartial trial.[1][4] He was executed by firing squad at Bagumbayan on 30 December 1896, aged thirty-five.[15] Constantino and others have argued that his execution contributed to the radicalisation of the independence movement, although the precise causal weight of this event remains a matter of historiographical debate.[3][8]

V

Character Analysis

Any assessment of Rizal’s character is necessarily interpretive, drawing on his correspondence, his contemporaries’ testimony, and the judgements of later biographers. The qualities summarised below are those most frequently identified in the scholarship; they are presented as supported interpretations rather than as settled fact.[4][5]

Virtues & Strengths

  • Intellectual range. Widely described as a polymath and polyglot.
  • Discipline. Relentless in study, work, and self-improvement.
  • Moral courage. Publicly criticised colonial abuses despite personal risk.
  • Compassion. Healed the poor and taught the young in exile.
  • Patriotism. Placed country above personal safety.
  • Reformism. Consistently favoured education and peaceful reform.

Shortcomings & Weaknesses

  • Reformist idealism. Some scholars argue he underestimated Spain’s resistance to reform.
  • Pride. Confidence in his own judgment could harden into stubbornness.
  • Distance from the revolution. His reservations about armed revolt drew criticism from contemporaries and later nationalists.
  • Sensitivity. Deeply affected by criticism and personal loss.
  • Restlessness. Frequent travel strained relationships and finances.

Evolution of Character Over Time

Biographers commonly periodise Rizal’s development across several phases: a privileged and academically successful youth; a student increasingly conscious of colonial inequity; a reformist writer during his European residence; a community practitioner during the Dapitan exile; and, finally, the condemned prisoner of 1896. Ocampo’s revisionist work in particular stresses the continuity between Rizal’s ordinary human experience and his later significance, cautioning against teleological readings that treat his eventual martyrdom as the predetermined fulfilment of his character.[5]

VI

Pitfalls & Heroism

A. Challenges & Obstacles

The biographical record documents a series of obstacles in Rizal’s life: institutional discrimination against Filipino students, the imprisonment of his mother and disputes affecting his family’s land tenure, the censorship and prohibition of his novels, his deportation to Dapitan, and his eventual trial before a military court.[4][9] These constitute the documented adversities against which his conduct may be assessed.

B. How These Became Heroism

A recurring argument in the scholarship is that Rizal responded to these adversities through sustained intellectual and civic activity rather than withdrawal: his experience of colonial injustice informed the social criticism of his novels, and his period of exile was marked by public works in Dapitan.[14] The interpretation of his execution is more contested. Nationalist historians, including Constantino, have examined the extent to which his death and posthumous veneration were appropriated by successive political authorities; the relationship between his martyrdom and the subsequent course of the revolution is therefore better stated as a subject of ongoing historiographical debate than as a settled conclusion.[3][8]

I die without seeing the dawn brighten over my native land.

— From “Mi Último Adiós,” attributed to Rizal, 1896 (trans.)[16]

VII

Conclusion

Summary of Findings

The evidence reviewed here suggests that Rizal’s development is most defensibly explained by environmental factors — his family’s social position, his formal and self-directed education, and the conditions of late Spanish colonial rule — rather than by inherited disposition, claims about which remain interpretive. The major biographies concur on the documented sequence of his life while differing in emphasis, and revisionist scholarship has productively complicated the inherited image of Rizal as a flawless national symbol.[3][5]

Reflection on His Legacy

Rizal’s legacy continues to be debated. He is officially commemorated as a national figure, yet scholars such as Constantino have questioned both the basis of that designation and the uses to which his memory has been put. A scholarly assessment therefore holds two propositions together: that Rizal’s reformist writing and conduct were historically consequential, and that his status as national hero is itself a constructed and contested historical phenomenon worthy of critical examination rather than uncritical veneration.[3]
